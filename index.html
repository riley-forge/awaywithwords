Design Recipe: Words Any Way

"The designs we take for granted are always historical and political"
- Anne Wysocki

By Riley Smith · Prep Time: 30 minutes of questioning your constraints
Cook Time: a lifetime of revising · Yield: one boldly designed text

Jump to Recipe

If you’d asked me last year what “serious” writing looked like, I would have responded with the answer my academic training has given me: double-spaced, 12pt font, Times New Roman, black text on white paper, with the occastional staple placed neatly in the top left corner. I very rarely thought of those things as design choices, and never questioned their decree from an academic perspective. They felt almost as constant as gravity; automatic, invisible, and non-negotiable. This format was what good students followed if they wanted their work to be taken seriously.

Then this course provided me with "awaywithwords" by Anne Wysocki, and it felt as though the page itself blossomed with options. She pointed out that the materials and shapes we’re “allowed” to use; fonts, margins, colors, and layouts close at hand for example, aren’t natural at all. These habits of formatting have been built over time, through institutions, technologies, and oftentimes power. Someone decided what academic writing should look like, and while we’ve all followed the recipe, we may have inadvertently hindered our capacity for creative communication.

So in this assignment, I’m inviting you into my rhetorical kitchen. Instead of a recipe for soup or cookies, we’re going to cook up a recipe for design, mixing awareness of constraints with a willingness to experiment with surfaces and shapes that academic writing often treats as unavailable. The dish at the end isn’t casserole or a cake; it’s a page that showcases its design instead of pretending to be neutral, presenting design as a feature, not a bug. Grab your metaphorical mixing bowl and your favorite font, and let’s start measuring out some ingredients.

Ingredients

  • 1 heaping cup of Constraint Awareness – notice what feels “automatic” in your writing: fonts, spacing, margins, and where images do or don’t appear.
  • 2 generous scoops of “Available Designs” – the recognizable patterns your audience already knows, like standard essays or slideshows.
  • 2 tablespoons of “Unavailable Designs” (room-temperature) – the choices that feel risky or out of place, such as playful layouts or nontraditional formats.
  • 1 pinch of Skepticism toward the Word/Image Split – a question about why words are treated as “serious” and images as “extra.”
  • A handful of Page-Space Experimentation – margins, line breaks, columns, and white space that change how your reader moves through the page.
  • 3 teaspoons of Audience Awareness – a clear sense of who you’re cooking for and what they expect your work to look like.
  • 1 cup of Rhetorical Purpose, firmly packed – a reason for composing that shapes which designs you keep and which you push against.
  • A dash of Institutional Flavor – assignment sheets, rubrics, style guides, and other official constraints you can’t fully ignore.
  • Optional garnish: courage to make the page look “wrong” on purpose – the willingness to design visibly and explain why.

Directions

  1. Preheat your context. Name your audience, purpose, and genre. Ask what this kind of writing usually looks like and whose expectations you’re working with.
  2. Sift together constraints and materials. List your materials (paper, screen, software, fonts, images) and your constraints (word count, formatting rules, deadlines), then decide which are truly fixed and which only feel automatic.
  3. Fold in your available designs. Add familiar patterns—clear titles, introductions, conclusions, or navigation bars—so your reader has something steady to hold onto.
  4. Stir in one or two unavailable designs. Introduce a design move that doesn’t usually appear in academic writing, such as a recipe format, unconventional headings, or hand-drawn elements that support your purpose. As Wysocki reminds us, page shapes are historical choices, not neutral defaults.
  5. Season with skepticism toward “neutral” pages. Taste your draft by asking where it pretends not to be designed. Decide whether you want the page to show its seams more honestly.
  6. Simmer with feedback. Share your draft with another reader and ask where their eyes go first, what feels clear, and what feels confusing.
  7. Adjust for ethics and accessibility. Check readability, color contrast, alt text, and overall inclusion, making sure your design invites in more readers than it shuts out.
  8. Plate your final design and serve with reflection. Present your work in its final form and write a brief reflection that explains how your design responds to your audience, purpose, and constraints.

Notes & Variations

Recipe Notes:

  • This recipe adapts to different genres: research papers, digital stories, discussion posts, and beyond.
  • Strong institutional flavor (strict formats) may limit some choices, but interesting design often happens in small details and margins.

Variations:

  • Minimalist: Use a calm layout but let one design choice be the focal voice, a bold pull quote or striking color are excellent options here!
  • Maximalist: Layer textures, images, and sidebars while making sure every element still serves your rhetorical purpose. Reading between the lines has never been so fun!
  • Remix: Take a traditional paper you’ve already planned out and rebuild it as a zine, slideshow, comic, or recipe blog. Oops! Did I do that?

Accessibility & Ethics

Good design isn’t just eye-catching; it’s responsible. As you experiment, remember to:

  • Choose fonts and sizes most readers can comfortably see.
  • Use enough contrast between text and background.
  • Add meaningful alt text to images so screen-reader users can access your work.
  • Consider whose voices and bodies your design centers and whether it welcomes multiple ways of reading and speaking.

Design is never neutral. The way your page looks can invite people in or quietly push them out. Ethical design asks who a page invites onto the surface and who gets pushed off the margins, often both literally and figuratively. Questioning ‘natural’ academic design widens who can participate in composing, and allows all chefs a place at the table!

About the Chef

Riley Smith is a student-writer who used to think design in writing required strict adherance if you wanted a shot, in academic circles at least! Writing could be much more fluid for your captive victims willing volunteers around the gaming table. However, after encountering ideas about how pages, screens, and bodies are shaped by culture and power, Riley has started bringing experiments in the rhetorical kitchen into her academic writing. Come along on a journey of compositional culinary discovery and joy!

When not revising essays, Riley enjoys writing with Obsidian and Zotero, running Draw Steel and other TTRPGs, and finding new ways to make writing feel like cooking with friends.

Behind This Recipe - Explanation Essay

This multimodal recipe blog reimagines Anne Wysocki’s discussion of design, constraints, and “available” vs. “unavailable” forms by translating theoretical concepts into the genre of a cooking recipe. The familiar structure—ingredients, directions, notes, and ads—helps readers recognize the genre even as the content focuses on composing practices instead of food.

When I began planning this project, I knew I wanted to challenge the assumption that academic writing should look a certain way; Times New Roman, black-and-white, centered on alphabetic text. After reading Anne Wysocki’s awaywithwords (2005), I realized those visual conventions are not neutral containers for ideas but historically shaped expectations about what counts as “serious.” Wysocki asks us to question why some designs become “available” and others seem inappropriate or unserious, despite being technically possible. My goal in composing this webpage as a recipe blog was to place design in the foreground as a central rhetorical decision rather than an invisible background.

The multimodal format of a recipe blog allows me to transform theoretical concepts into something tactile. Instead of merely describing design principles, I wanted the page to model how material choices communicate, incorporating layout, spacing, color, headings, and even the presence of "advertisements", though those just lead to other course materials for now. For instance, the recipe card translates abstract ideas into “ingredients,” demonstrating how rhetorical elements like audience and institutional constraints can be measured and mixed. The jump-to-recipe button and accessible figcaptions highlight how genre conventions guide movement throughout the page, while the aforementioned ads reference other course texts to suggest a broader pantry of rhetorical resources.

My audience for this project is my peers, who already share experience reading multimodal theory. For that reason, I wasn’t trying to simplify Wysocki but to make her ideas more experiential. The recipe metaphor suggests that composition is crafted, mixed, and continually tasted rather than automatically formatted, ideally focusing the reader's perspective on design as process rather than final product. Ultimately, this project is meant to function as its own example of an “unavailable design” inside academic expectations, an argument that the page is never neutral and that rhetorical understanding depends on recognizing design not as ornamentation, but as an essential component of meaning.

References

Wysocki, A. F. (2005). awaywithwords: On the possibilities in unavailable designs. Computers and Composition, 22(1), 55–62. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.compcom.2004.12.011
